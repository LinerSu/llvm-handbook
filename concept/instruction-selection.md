# Instruction Selection

> üß≠ **Concept** ¬∑ `concept ¬∑ codegen ¬∑ llvm` ¬∑ Index [[LLVM.MOC]] ¬∑ see also [[dragon-book-ch8.MOC|Dragon Ch.8]]
> **Prerequisites:** [[code-generation-overview]] ¬∑ **Followed by:** [[register-allocation]]

> [!abstract] Chapter map
> Instruction selection maps **target-independent IR operations** to **target machine instructions**. One IR op may need several machine instructions, and one machine instruction may cover several IR ops ‚Äî so selection is a **covering** (tiling) problem. LLVM has three selectors: **SelectionDAG** (default), **GlobalISel** (newer), and **FastISel** (`-O0`).

> [!info] The textbook framing: tiling
> Represent the computation as a tree/DAG; a target instruction is a **tile** (a small pattern with a cost) that covers part of it. Selecting code = covering the whole tree with tiles at minimum total cost. **Maximal munch** greedily takes the largest matching tile; **dynamic programming** finds an *optimal* tiling. LLVM's pattern matchers are the engineering form of this.

---

## 1. SelectionDAG (the default)

For each basic block, LLVM builds a **DAG** of operations, then runs four phases:

1. **Build** ‚Äî IR ‚Üí an `SDNode` DAG (shared subexpressions are shared, like an [[three-address-code|expression DAG]]).
2. **Legalize** ‚Äî rewrite types and operations the target can't handle (e.g. promote `i1`‚Üí`i8`, expand a 64-bit op on a 32-bit target).
3. **Combine** ‚Äî peephole-style DAG simplifications (folded between phases).
4. **Select** ‚Äî **pattern-match** target instruction tiles onto the DAG. The patterns and their costs are generated by **TableGen** from the target's `.td` files ‚Äî this is the tiling step.

A final **scheduler** linearizes the selected DAG into a sequence of `MachineInstr`s.

> [!example] Tiling, concretely
> An IR `load` then `add` (`%v = load ptr %p; %r = add %v, %x`) may match a single x86 tile `add %x, (%p)` (a load-and-add addressing-mode instruction) ‚Äî one tile covering two IR ops ‚Äî if the target defines that pattern. On a RISC target with no memory operands, it stays two instructions (`ld`, `add`).

## 2. GlobalISel (whole-function, newer)

GlobalISel replaces the per-block DAG with a function-wide pipeline on **generic MIR (gMIR)**:

```mermaid
flowchart LR
  IR["LLVM IR"] --> T["IRTranslator"] --> L["Legalizer"] --> R["RegBankSelect"] --> S["InstructionSelect"] --> M["target MIR"]
```

- **IRTranslator** ‚Äî IR ‚Üí generic `G_*` MIR opcodes.
- **Legalizer** ‚Äî make those generic ops legal for the target.
- **RegBankSelect** ‚Äî assign each value a register *bank* (e.g. GPR vs. FPR).
- **InstructionSelect** ‚Äî match generic ops to real target instructions (TableGen patterns, shared with SelectionDAG where possible).

It avoids SelectionDAG's per-block isolation and large `SDNode` graphs, and is most mature on AArch64.

## 3. FastISel (`-O0`)

A fast, **best-effort** selector for unoptimized builds: it handles common cases directly for compile speed and **falls back to SelectionDAG** for anything it can't select.

> [!summary] The one thing to remember
> Instruction selection is **tiling**: cover the IR with target-instruction patterns at low cost. LLVM does it three ways ‚Äî per-block **SelectionDAG** (build‚Üílegalize‚Üícombine‚Üíselect, TableGen patterns), whole-function **GlobalISel** (translate‚Üílegalize‚Üíregbank‚Üíselect), and quick **FastISel** at `-O0`.

> [!warning] Version-sensitive
> Which selector is the default is **target- and version-dependent**: SelectionDAG remains the default on most targets while GlobalISel is the default on some (most mature on AArch64). Confirm for your target/version in [CodeGenerator.html](https://llvm.org/docs/CodeGenerator.html) (this vault tracks [[llvm-version]]).
